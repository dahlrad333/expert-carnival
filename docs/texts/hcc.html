HCC Scout

  • Summary

    A suite of web and desktop applications leveraging natural language processing to enable health insurance providers to data-mine patient medical records for opportunities to better document illnesses and injuries.

  • Methods

    Service design, shadowing, contextual interviews, data analysis, prototyping, usability testing, unit testing, acceptance testing, RESTful APIs, natural language processing

  • Clients

    UPMC Health Plan, Health Fidelity

THE PROBLEM

For many patients (e.g., those covered by Medicare), their health insurance provider is reimbursed some money by the government based on how sick the patient is. The provider is responsible for tracking and reporting this information, but the government has strict requirements for what medical documentation is needed to submit an illness for reimbursement.

Failing to submit all of the relevant illnesses, or have the right evidence to back them up, can mean millions of lost revenue. So providers employ special staff to review the documentation to ensure nothing was missed, either by the provider when submitting, or even by the doctors when writing their clinical notes. This extra layer of security is expensive, but worthwhile if it can be made extremely efficient.

THE SOLUTION

HCC Scout uses natural language processing and complex algorithms to analyze the various illnesses and injuries mentioned in a patient's clinical notes. When a missed opportunity for reimbursement is discovered, the relevant documents are sent to an expert team for confirmation.

Users instantly see exactly what was discovered alongside potential evidence, allowing them to perform their reviews quickly and efficiently. Managers can use the administrative dashboard to track performance, manage their team, and prioritize incoming opportunities.



THE PROCESS

Research

Finding opportunities for reimbursement involves reading hundreds of documents a day, looking for evidence of illnesses not already submitted to the government. This involves coordination between a wide network of individuals, including doctors, administrative staff, IT, business analysts, and (of course) coding experts.

My contributions to the solution were primarily interfacing the natural language processing to the RESTful backend. The backend would ingest a daily report of new documents that had been digitally imaged. These documents would be tested for potential missing HL7 codes. The backend would in turn drive the front end for the [human] medial coders, highlighting these opportunities. In addition, I would also consume wireframes from designers and translate them into functional webpages.

Analyzing Return on Investment

To gain support (read: funding) for the product, my team first needed to prove its potential. Working with business analysts, we built an extremely rough "Wizard of Oz"-esque prototype using Excel and a small sample of previously-reviewed documents. This tiny A/B test showed signs of potential, and the project was funded.

Throughout development, the team released working, alpha versions periodically to continue testing if we were on the right track. These functional prototypes were fantastic for testing usability and getting insightful feedback from users.
